What should we call this?
A naming analysis

Hard Power Project vs Common Power

Hard Power Project has real energy and communicates the stakes. But it carries pre-loaded meaning that actively conflicts with the project's purpose — and two of the most critical funders are either hostile to it or friction-generating. Common Power resolves that problem but risks sounding too gentle for a project that wants to blow doors off.

Where the group has landed

Session 4 agreed Hard Power Project as a working title, with the logic that intentional friction filters for the right people: those who grasp how precarious the situation is. The tagline was flagged as equally critical as the name. Since then, Rich's funder testing has complicated the picture significantly.

Strategic read

The group's instinct is defensible. Intentional friction can work. But there is a meaningful difference between friction that attracts the right people and friction that creates an immediate rejection response in the room you most need to win over.

Halima Khan is not a fringe view. She chairs the group of funder CEOs the project needs to convene. Her response to the name is not a persuasion problem to be managed with a better tagline. It is a structural signal that the name may cost more than the friction earns.

This does not resolve cleanly in either direction. But the team should go into the QCF meeting on 27 May having made a deliberate choice — not drifting forward on a working title that was never formally decided.

All options considered
Option A — current working title
Hard Power Project
For
  • Communicates serious intent immediately. No ambiguity about stakes.
  • Intentional friction filters for the right people: those who grasp how precarious the problem is.
  • Adding "Project" creates useful distance from the geopolitical definition and signals time-boundedness.
  • Indy: soft names attract soft rooms. If it's crap you learn quickly and iterate.
  • A strong tagline can carry interpretation and sharpen the name's meaning.
  • Clare: naming it, then killing it — the absence of the name at wind-down also becomes useful.
Against
  • "Hard power" has a well-established definition: military force and economic coercion. Utterly at odds with what this project does.
  • Halima Khan: "I really don't like hard power."
  • Tom Steinberg: will put off people spending time protesting.
  • Alex: you will spend a lot of time saying "no, no, it's not that" — a waste given the pace required.
  • People like Alex Karp are now using the term. Gives the ick.
  • Rich: generating too much friction too quickly. Will need a very good reason to proceed.
Option B
Common Power
For
  • Locates the project in ground-up collaboration rather than coercive force.
  • Readily understood without a lengthy explainer.
  • Situates the work in a commons framing — scope for cross-sector alignment (knowledge, food, creative commons).
  • Keeps "power" in the name, important for power-literacy in the field.
  • Less likely to trigger a rejection response in funder meetings.
  • Halima Khan: "much better than hard power."
Against
  • Risks sounding too gentle for a project with sharp intent.
  • Clare: sounds a bit nice. May create a false impression of what is actually being done.
  • Indy: may attract "egalitarian VC types" who can turn good projects bad.
  • Vague. Does not communicate urgency or structural seriousness.
  • A US organisation uses a similar name. Not a deal-breaker but worth noting.
Option C — floated session 3
Hard Power (no "Project")
For
  • Simpler. More direct. Indy: good names are simple.
  • Speaks clearly to the limitations of the current movement — insufficiently hard.
Against
  • Without "Project" it loses the time-limited signal that distances it from the geopolitical definition.
  • Harder to fund — "hard power" alone sounds more confrontational to institutional funders.
  • Clare: missing a hook or object.
Option D — floated session 3
Civic Power (rejected)
For
  • Softer. Removes friction with funders entirely.
  • Already familiar language in the philanthropic space.
Against
  • Already taken — QCF funds the Civic Power Fund. Indy: "we can't use civic power."
  • Does not differentiate this project from existing work in the field at all.
  • Loses the point entirely. The whole argument is that civic is insufficient.
Option E — floated session 3, not developed
Verbing the name

Indy floated intentionally using a verb rather than a noun — "hard powering," "folk powering" — so the name describes an action rather than an object. The logic: avoids creating a brand identity that can be attacked or co-opted, keeps the focus on doing rather than being. Not developed further.

Ephemeral / unnamed

Mara raised the possibility of resisting naming altogether — operating with intentional obscurity, sharing details on a need-to-know basis. Indy's response: viable for a persistent institution, but for a time-limited one, a name that can be killed is more useful than no name. Not pursued.

The unresolved problem

The tagline question is arguably as important as the name itself and has not been closed. Multiple people said a strong strapline could rescue either option. No tagline has been formally agreed. This gap should be closed at the same time as the name decision.

The structural problem

The name says Hard Power. The current tagline says civic power. A reader will notice and wonder which one describes the project. This is not accidental — civic is softer, more fundable — but it creates incoherence at the top of the document that the body text then has to resolve.

If Hard Power Project stays, the tagline needs to earn the name rather than soften it. If Common Power becomes the name, the tagline needs to do sharpening work rather than softening.
